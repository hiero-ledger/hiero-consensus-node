# Strongly-seeing

## Where we are

This is the third lesson in cluster A.1 (Hashgraph Algorithm). [A.1-1 the hashgraph DAG](A.1-1-hashgraph-dag.md) defined the data structure; [A.1-2 rounds and witnesses](A.1-2-rounds-and-witnesses.md) wrote a *round-created* number onto every vertex and identified *witnesses* as the events that mark a round transition. That lesson treated the strongly-seeing relation as a black box — a function that, given an event and a witness round, said how many witnesses of that round the event strongly sees. This lesson opens the box. The two lessons that follow consume what we open today: [A.1-4 voting and coin rounds](A.1-4-voting-and-coin-rounds.md) tallies counting votes over the witnesses each voter strongly sees, and [A.1-5 judges](A.1-5-judges.md) collapses the resulting famous witnesses to one per creator. Strongly-seeing is the primitive both round assignment and fame voting test against.

## Motivating problem

The hashgraph algorithm needs a per-event, per-witness predicate that asks: "is this event downstream of enough of the network's view of that witness?" Two different parts of the algorithm consult the predicate. Round assignment needs it to decide round bumps — case 4 of `ConsensusImpl.round` (covered in [A.1-2](A.1-2-rounds-and-witnesses.md#how-it-works)) only bumps round when an event strongly sees a super-majority of weight on the witnesses of the parent round. Fame voting (next lesson) needs it to tally counting votes: a witness's vote on a candidate witness one or more rounds earlier is computed over the prior-round witnesses *the voter strongly sees*. Both tests must be Byzantine-safe.

A naive "does x descend from y at all?" predicate is not safe. It admits any single ancestral path; a Byzantine creator branching on its self-line could be exploited to fabricate apparent agreement among intermediates that are in fact one creator wearing many hats. The hashgraph's answer is to require *distinct-creator weight* on the path — a super-majority of independent voices have to all see the same witness. The mechanics that implement that turn out to be more involved than the plain-language version because they have to deal with creators that branch (publish multiple events from the same self-line). That gap is what this lesson covers.

## Concept

The conceptual definition is at [`concepts/strongly-seeing.md`](../../concepts/strongly-seeing.md). Read its *Definition* and *Mechanics* sections before continuing.

The two framings to anchor on:

- **Plain language.** Event *x* *strongly sees* witness *y* iff there is a set of intermediate events — each by a distinct creator, collectively a super-majority of roster weight — that are all both descendants of *y* and ancestors of *x*. A super-majority of independent creators all "see" *y* on the path between *y* and *x*.
- **Code-level.** The implementation flips the question: rather than starting with witness *y* and counting paths, it starts with target member *m* and asks "what witness in the parent round of *x* is *x*'s strongly-seen witness for member *m*?" The answer is either a specific `EventImpl` (the canonical witness by *m* on which a super-majority of intermediates' lastSee chains agree) or null. The boolean predicate is `stronglySeeP(x, m) != null`.

Two structural facts these framings impose. First, strongly-seeing is *anti-monotonic in branching*: a creator that branches its self-line splits the per-creator agreement and reduces, not increases, the weight that can be counted toward super-majority. The relation is robust against Byzantine self-fork attacks for that reason. Second, strongly-seeing is *memoizable per event and per member*: once `stronglySeeP(x, m)` is computed for one *m*, the algorithm computes it for every *m* of the roster in the same pass and writes the answers to per-event slots. The DAG walk underlying the relation is expensive enough that the memoization is load-bearing for performance — the wrapper that times it (next subsection) is the metric the team uses to track that cost.

## How it works

The strongly-seeing primitive lives in `ConsensusImpl` and rests on two helpers — `lastSee` and `seeThru` — that themselves walk the DAG. We take them in order: `lastSee` is the foundation, `seeThru` composes two `lastSee` lookups, and `stronglySeeP` aggregates `seeThru` results across the roster.

`lastSee(x, m)` at [`ConsensusImpl.java#L965`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L965) returns the most recent event by roster member *m* that is an ancestor of *x*. The function is memoized aggressively: the first call with a given *x* initialises slots for every member of the roster (`x.initLastSee(rosterLookup.numMembers())`), computes the answer for every *m*, and stores them all. Subsequent calls with the same *x* — for any *m* — read directly from the slot. The aggressive policy reflects that the algorithm queries `lastSee(x, *)` across all members during a single round-assignment or fame-voting pass on *x*; computing one and discarding the per-parent recursion would cost almost the same as computing all and amortising the recursion.

The recursion has three cases per *m*. If *x*'s creator is *m*, *x* sees itself and the slot is set to *x*. If *x* has no parents, the slot is null. Otherwise, for every non-ancient parent of *x*, the function recurses — `lastSee(parent, m)` — and picks the candidate with the highest `round()` value. A tiebreaker on the per-event `DeGen` slot disambiguates when two candidates tie on round and their parents share a `firstSee` answer; `DeGen` is the algorithm's internal generation counter introduced later in cluster A.1, and its purpose here is to handle the deterministic merge across forked self-lines.

`seeThru(x, m, m2)` at [`ConsensusImpl.java#L1028`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L1028) returns the witness by member *m* that *x* sees through some event by member *m2*. This is the composition that strongly-seeing turns on. In the general case `seeThru(x, m, m2)` is `firstSee(lastSee(x, m2), m)` — find the most recent event by *m2* that *x* descends from, then take the first witness by *m* reachable forward from that event. The diagonal special case at [line 1035](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L1035) handles the case where *m* equals *m2* and *x* is itself by member *m*: the function returns the first self-witness reachable from *x*'s self-parent. This case is what gives the function a *canonical* anchor — the answer the algorithm subsequently asks every other member's lastSee chain to agree with.

`stronglySeeP(x, m)` at [`ConsensusImpl.java#L1054`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L1054) is the strongly-seeing primitive proper. Like `lastSee` it is aggressively memoized — one pass computes the answer for every *m* of the roster and writes to per-event slots — and it returns either a specific witness (when super-majority is met) or null.

Per member *mm*, the computation has two paths:

- *Inheritance.* Lines [1075–1080](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L1075). For each non-ancient parent of *x*, if that parent strongly sees through *mm* (`stronglySeeP(parent, mm) != null`) and *x* has the same parent round as that parent (`parentRound(parent) == prx`), *x* inherits the parent's strongly-see slot for *mm* and skips the full computation. The argument: when *x* and parent share a parent round, the round-r witnesses-seen-through-intermediates by parent are also seen-through-intermediates by *x* (because *x* descends from parent), so any super-majority parent has met, *x* has met too — through the same canonical witness.
- *Full computation.* Lines [1083–1100](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L1083). The canonical witness by *mm* is `st = seeThru(x, mm, mm)` — the witness by *mm* reached through *mm*'s own lastSee from *x*. If `round(st) != prx` (the canonical exists but is in the wrong round), the slot is set to null. Otherwise the function walks every member *m3* of the roster, tests `seeThru(x, mm, m3) == st`, and accumulates the weight of every *m3* whose lastSee chain through *mm* agrees on the same canonical witness. The accumulated weight is tested against `Threshold.SUPER_MAJORITY.isSatisfiedBy(weight, totalWeight)` ([`Threshold.java#L22`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/base-utility/src/main/java/org/hiero/base/utility/Threshold.java#L22) — strictly more than 2/3 of total weight). On success, the slot is set to the canonical witness; on failure, null.

The canonical-witness condition is the Byzantine-safe core. A creator *m* that branches on its self-line produces multiple candidate witnesses by *m* in the same round. Different intermediates' `lastSee(., m)` chains may resolve to different forks. Only intermediates whose `seeThru(x, m, m3)` matches the *one* canonical anchor `seeThru(x, m, m)` — itself rooted in *x*'s own self-parent path — are counted toward super-majority. A branching creator splits its own contribution across forks and lowers, never raises, the weight tally. This is what makes "super-majority of distinct-creator weight" Byzantine-robust where naive descendant-counting would not be.

`timedStronglySeeP(x, m)` at [`ConsensusImpl.java#L880`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L880) is a thin metric-collecting wrapper around `stronglySeeP`. It records `System.nanoTime()` around the call and feeds the delta to `consensusMetrics.dotProductTime(t)` — the "dot product time" metric the team watches as a proxy for how much wall time the algorithm spends in strongly-seeing. The function comment frames the cost informally as "a kind of generalized dot product"; over the strongly-seen relation across all members, every round-assignment pass on *x* amounts to a small matrix-vector product that is the algorithm's dominant CPU cost. The wrapper is the only call site `round()` uses; recursive internal calls invoke `stronglySeeP` directly to avoid double-counting time.

Two places in the consensus pipeline consume `stronglySeeP`:

- *Round assignment.* `ConsensusImpl.round` case 4, walked in [A.1-2](A.1-2-rounds-and-witnesses.md#how-it-works). When parents agree on round *r*, the function counts roster weight of round-*r* witnesses *x* strongly sees and bumps to *r + 1* iff super-majority is met. The strongly-seen check is the gate.
- *Counting votes.* `ConsensusImpl.getCountingVote` ([A.1-4 voting and coin rounds](A.1-4-voting-and-coin-rounds.md), upcoming) tallies YES/NO vote weight over the prior-round witnesses the voter strongly sees. The same primitive, the same threshold, applied to a different per-witness boolean.

Both consumers ask the same predicate of every witness in a particular round and threshold the total weight against `Threshold.SUPER_MAJORITY`. The architecture topic frames both at [`hashgraph.md#algorithm-in-current-code`](../../architecture/topics/hashgraph.md#algorithm-in-current-code).

## Worked example

Four equal-weight nodes A, B, C, D. The super-majority threshold is strictly more than 2/3 of total weight; with weights `1+1+1+1 = 4`, that is strictly more than `8/3 ≈ 2.67`, so 3 of 4 satisfies and 2 of 4 does not.

Suppose round 1 has four witnesses, one per creator: `y_A`, `y_B`, `y_C`, `y_D`. Now consider an event *x* by A in some later position — it does not matter for this example whether *x* is itself a round-1 event or a round-2 candidate, only that its parent round is `r = 1`. The round-assignment pass on *x* will compute `stronglySeeP(x, m)` for each `m ∈ {A, B, C, D}`, sum roster weight where it returns non-null, and test the total against super-majority.

Take the case `stronglySeeP(x, A)` — does *x* strongly see *some* witness by A in round 1? Compute the canonical first: `st = seeThru(x, A, A)`. Because A is *x*'s own creator, the diagonal special case at `ConsensusImpl.java` line 1035 fires and `st` is the first self-witness reachable from *x*'s self-parent — concretely, `y_A` (A's only round-1 witness, given no branching).

Now walk the per-member tally. For each `m3 ∈ {A, B, C, D}`, the function asks `seeThru(x, A, m3) == st`, accumulating *m3*'s weight on a match:

- `seeThru(x, A, A)` is `st` by construction. Weight 1 counted.
- `seeThru(x, A, B)` is `firstSee(lastSee(x, B), A)` — the first witness by A reached from the most recent ancestor of *x* by B. If *x* has gossiped with B since `y_A` and B's lastSee chain runs through `y_A`, this returns `y_A`. Weight 1 counted.
- `seeThru(x, A, C)` and `seeThru(x, A, D)` resolve symmetrically: if *x* has gossiped with C and D and their lastSee chains pass through `y_A`, both return `y_A`. Weight 2 more counted.

Total weight 4. `Threshold.SUPER_MAJORITY.isSatisfiedBy(4, 4)` is true (4 > 8/3). The slot is set to `y_A` and the boolean predicate `stronglySeeP(x, A) != null` is true.

Repeating for `m ∈ {B, C, D}` symmetrically, *x* strongly sees `y_B`, `y_C`, `y_D` in this scenario. Round assignment sums roster weight over `{A, B, C, D}` (weight 4 total) and tests against super-majority (4 > 8/3, true). Round bumps from 1 to 2; *x* is a round-2 witness for A.

Now alter the scenario: B has *not* yet gossiped with D since round 1, so *x* has no ancestral path to D's view of round 1 through any intermediate other than D itself. `seeThru(x, A, D)` falls back to a lastSee chain that does not include `y_A` — perhaps to a pre-`y_A` event by D, in which case `firstSee(., A)` returns either an earlier-round A-witness or null. The tally for `stronglySeeP(x, A)` drops to 3 of 4: A, B, C agree on `y_A`, D does not. `3 > 8/3` is still true, super-majority still met, *x* still strongly sees `y_A`. But if a *second* member's intermediates also fail to agree, the tally drops to 2 of 4. `2 > 8/3` is false. The slot is null and *x* does not strongly see `y_A` — even though `y_A` is unambiguously an ancestor of *x*.

This is the case that motivated the canonical-witness condition: ancestral existence is not enough; *agreement of a super-majority of distinct members' lastSee chains on the same canonical witness* is the gate. In the contrived branching variant where A has published a fork `y_A'`, half the intermediates' lastSee chains may resolve to `y_A'` instead of `y_A`. The `seeThru(x, A, m3) == st` comparison rejects every intermediate whose lastSee through A landed on `y_A'` (since `st` is anchored to *x*'s self-parent path), and the branching creator's contribution toward super-majority collapses.
