# Judges

## Where we are

This is the fifth lesson in cluster A.1 (Hashgraph Algorithm). [A.1-1 the hashgraph DAG](A.1-1-hashgraph-dag.md) defined the data structure; [A.1-2 rounds and witnesses](A.1-2-rounds-and-witnesses.md) labelled events with a round-created and identified the per-creator round transitions called *witnesses*; [A.1-3 strongly-seeing](A.1-3-strongly-seeing.md) opened the predicate that round assignment and fame voting both lean on; [A.1-4 voting and coin rounds](A.1-4-voting-and-coin-rounds.md) drove the voting machinery that decides each witness's fame. This lesson takes the famous-witness verdicts that fall out of A.1-4 and turns them into a *judge set*: one famous witness per creator, deterministically chosen, sorted by creator id. The judges fix round-received for the events that reach consensus in that round, seed the per-round whitening string used by the consensus sort, and become the persisted handle by which a snapshot replays the round on restart or reconnect. The next lesson [A.1-6 event lifecycle and birth round](A.1-6-event-lifecycle-and-birth-round.md) walks the event staircase the `EventWindow` carried out of this lesson gates.

## Motivating problem

A.1-4 ended on the moment `RoundElections.isDecided()` flipped true: every witness in the election round has a fame verdict. That is not yet enough to emit a round. Three problems remain.

First, a Byzantine creator can produce more than one famous witness in the same round by branching its self-line — A.1-4 walked the case where A's fork produces both `w_A` and `w_A'` and both end up decided famous. If the algorithm fed both into the round-received logic, the *round received* assignment for ancestor events would be ambiguous: an event could be an ancestor of `w_A` but not `w_A'`, and the question "did this event reach consensus in round 5?" would have no Byzantine-safe answer.

Second, even with a unique famous witness per creator, the algorithm needs a deterministic order across the consensus events that those judges fix. The order is what every honest node has to agree on — exactly. Two independent runs against the same DAG must produce the same `List<PlatformEvent>` in the same sequence, including how the algorithm breaks ties between events that share a preliminary consensus timestamp.

Third, the round must be persisted in a form that survives restart, snapshot replay, and reconnect. The snapshot's commitment cannot be the full DAG — by the time a node loads a snapshot, the events that reached consensus in the snapshot's round may already have been pruned. The snapshot's commitment is the round's *judges*, identified by creator id and base hash. On restart, the local DAG re-walk must recognise judges from old rounds and rehydrate their `isJudge` flag without re-running fame voting.

The judges, as defined in [`concepts/judges.md`](../../concepts/judges.md), solve all three. This lesson walks the mechanics: how the famous set collapses, how the resulting judges fix round-received, how the whitening string they produce closes the consensus sort, and how `JudgeId` is the bridge between in-memory consensus state and the on-disk snapshot.

## Concept

The conceptual definition lives at [`concepts/judges.md`](../../concepts/judges.md); read it before continuing if you have not.

The four framings to anchor on:

- **One famous witness per creator.** The judge set of round `r` is the famous-witness set of round `r` collapsed by creator id, with the deterministic tie-breaker that an honest creator never trips. A creator who never branches contributes at most one famous witness per round automatically; a creator who branches contributes one judge, picked by minimum base hash. The collapse is what makes the round-received predicate well-defined regardless of branching.
- **Round-received via common ancestry.** An event reaches consensus in round `r` iff it is an ancestor of *every* judge of `r` and was not already consensus in some earlier round and is not ancient. The judges of `r` jointly close the round; they do not individually close it. An event that is an ancestor of all but one judge waits for a later round whose judge set includes a creator whose judge in `r` it does not yet descend from.
- **Whitening seeds the consensus sort's final tie-break.** [`concepts/judges.md`](../../concepts/judges.md) names the round's whitening string — the XOR of the judge base hashes — and notes it participates in the consensus order tie-break. The mechanics are in [A.1-4](A.1-4-voting-and-coin-rounds.md): the whitening byte array is XOR-ed into each event's base hash before the lexicographic compare, so the bit pattern that determines the per-pair winner is unpredictable until the round's judges are known. An adversary who shapes events to manipulate the lex-order in advance has nothing stable to target.
- **Judges are the snapshot's commitment.** The on-disk `ConsensusSnapshot` does not carry events; it carries `JudgeId` records — `(creator_id, base_hash)` per judge — alongside the round number, the minimum-judge-info for each round in the rolling window, the running `numConsensus` counter, and the `lastConsensusTime`. On restart and reconnect (covered in [A.1-3 strongly-seeing](A.1-3-strongly-seeing.md#how-it-works) at altitude, deep in cluster C later), `Consensus.loadSnapshot` seeds an `InitJudges` instance with the judge hashes of the last decided round; as events flow back into the DAG, hashes that match a stored judge get the flag re-applied without rerunning fame voting.

A structural note these framings impose. The judges *are* the consensus output of the round in the sense that mattered for replay: round number plus judges plus minimum-judge-info plus consensus timestamp uniquely determines the consensus events of that round given a DAG. The events themselves can be re-derived; the judges cannot, because their identity depends on a fame voting history that prior events drove and that restart deletes. That asymmetry is why the snapshot persists judges rather than persisting consensus events.

## How it works

`roundDecided` at [`ConsensusImpl.java#L706`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L706) is the entry point. It is called from `addEvent` ([line 424](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L424)) the first time `RoundElections.isDecided()` returns true on a round, after `voteInAllElections` has flipped the last undecided witness's fame and provided the engine is not waiting for init judges. The call returns a fully-built `ConsensusRound`; the caller appends it to the engine's output and continues processing events.

The collapse from famous witnesses to judges happens inside `RoundElections.findAllJudges` at [`RoundElections.java#L136`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/RoundElections.java#L136). The method walks the `elections` list of `CandidateWitness` entries, skips any not flagged famous, and merges the survivors into a `Map<NodeId, EventImpl>` keyed by creator id, using `RoundElections::uniqueFamous` ([line 174](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/RoundElections.java#L174)) as the merge function. `uniqueFamous` picks the entry whose `getBaseHash().getBytes()` is lexicographically least under signed-byte comparison — `ArrayUtils.arrayCompare`. The lex-min rule is unconditional and entirely local; every honest node looking at the same set of famous witnesses arrives at the same judge.

The collapsed map's values are copied into a list, the list is sorted by `getCreatorId().id()` ascending ([line 154](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/RoundElections.java#L154)), and the method walks the sorted list once more to set `minNGen` and `minBirthRound` (one pair per round, used downstream for ancient-threshold maintenance) and to call `EventImpl.setJudgeTrue` ([`EventImpl.java#L163`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/EventImpl.java#L163)) on each judge. The flag is the per-event handle later code uses — `ConsensusRounds.isLastDecidedJudge` ([line 158](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusRounds.java#L158)) and `InitJudges.judgeFound` ([line 64](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/InitJudges.java#L64)) both lean on it — and is cleared in `EventImpl.clear` when the event becomes ancient. `findAllJudges` throws if the result is empty; the caller does not catch.

Back in `roundDecided`, the just-returned judge list flows into three downstream computations. `checkJudges` at [`ConsensusImpl.java#L774`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L774) sums the roster weights of the judges and tests `Threshold.SUPER_MAJORITY.isSatisfiedBy(judgeWeights, rosterTotalWeight)`. Failure is logged via `noSuperMajorityLogger.error` and is *not* fatal — the round still emits. The same is true of an empty judges list (`noJudgeLogger.error`). The error logs flag invariant breaches, not termination conditions; in current code the consensus pipeline keeps running. SME context on the soft-fail choice is in Open questions.

`ConsensusUtils.generateWhitening` at [`ConsensusUtils.java#L53`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusUtils.java#L53) walks the judge list and XOR-folds each judge's base hash into a fresh byte array of length `CryptoConstants.SIG_SIZE_BYTES`. The result is the round's whitening string; it has the same length as a single hash and is symmetric over judge order, so the lex-sort by creator id done earlier in `findAllJudges` is structural for traceability rather than required for correctness here.

`findConsensusEvents` at [`ConsensusImpl.java#L804`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L804) is where round-received is fixed. It calls `search.commonAncestorsOf(judges, this::nonConsensusNonAncient)` ([line 807](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L807)) — an ancestor walk that returns events ancestor to *every* judge in the input list, filtered to events that are not yet consensus and not ancient. Each returned event has `setIsConsensusTrue(e, decidedRound)` invoked on it; that sets `roundReceived = decidedRound`, computes `preliminaryConsensusTimestamp` from the per-event `recTimes` (one timestamp per voter at which the voter saw the event become an ancestor of a judge), and flags the event as consensus. Events that are an ancestor of a strict subset of the judges are not in the return set — they wait for a later round whose judges they fully ancestor.

The list of just-consensus events then goes through `ConsensusSorter.sort(consensus, whitening)` ([line 814](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L814)), which assigns `cGen` to each event via `LocalConsensusGeneration.assignCGen`, sorts by the four-key compare (preliminary consensus timestamp → extended median over `recTimes` → `cGen` → whitened base-hash compare via `ArrayUtils.arrayCompare(h1, h2, whitening)`), then clears `cGen`. The whitening only matters when the first three keys all tie — by construction extremely rare and confined to events whose preliminary timestamps and median timestamps and `cGen` all agree. The point of involving the whitening at all is that the tie-break input must be unpredictable to an adversary at event-creation time.

Finally, `roundDecided` packages the result. The judge list is mapped into `JudgeId` records — `creator_id` plus `base_hash` per judge — and the records are folded into a `ConsensusSnapshot` along with the round number, the minimum-judge-info list (which carries `(round, minBirthRound)` for every round in the rolling ancient window), the running `numConsensus` counter, and `lastConsensusTime` ([lines 745–763](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L745)). The snapshot is wrapped inside a `ConsensusRound` carrying the consensus-event list, the `EventWindow` that defines the new ancient and expired thresholds and the next pending round, the active roster, the `pcesMode` flag, and a creation timestamp. That `ConsensusRound` is the single value that flows out of the consensus topic on `consensusRoundOutputWire` and downstream into signed-state management.

The reverse direction — replay — is handled by `InitJudges` at [`InitJudges.java`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/InitJudges.java). On `Consensus.loadSnapshot`, the snapshot's last decided round and its `judgeIds` populate an `InitJudges` instance whose `judgeHashes` set holds the expected base hashes. As the linker rehydrates events from PCES or from reconnect, each non-ancient event with a hash in the set has `judgeFound(judge)` invoked — that single call sets the event's `roundCreated`, marks it witness, marks it famous, marks it fame-decided, and sets the judge flag. Once `numMissingJudges()` reaches zero, the engine has reconstructed enough state to keep running consensus from the next pending round; until then, `Consensus.waitingForInitJudges()` returns true and `addEvent` returns an empty output.

## Worked example

Continue the four-equal-weight nodes A, B, C, D scenario A.1-4 closed on. A is Byzantine and branched its self-line in round 5: both `w_A` and the fork witness `w_A'` end up decided famous after voting completes around round 18 (after the round-17 coin round broke the deadlock). B, C, D each contributed one famous witness per creator: `w_B`, `w_C`, `w_D`.

`RoundElections.isDecided()` returns true on round 5 — every candidate witness in `elections` is decided. `voteInAllElections` returns; `addEvent` calls `roundDecided(roundElections)`.

`findAllJudges` walks the elections list. `w_A`, `w_A'`, `w_B`, `w_C`, `w_D` are all `isFamous() == true`. The merge into the `Map<NodeId, EventImpl>`:

- `w_B` → key B, value `w_B`. No conflict; merge takes the new entry directly.
- `w_C` → key C, value `w_C`. Same.
- `w_D` → key D, value `w_D`. Same.
- `w_A` → key A, value `w_A`. First entry for A; merge takes it.
- `w_A'` → key A, value already `w_A`. Merge calls `uniqueFamous(w_A, w_A')`. The function compares `w_A.getBaseHash().getBytes()` and `w_A'.getBaseHash().getBytes()` with `ArrayUtils.arrayCompare`. Suppose `w_A`'s hash is lexicographically less. The map keeps `w_A`.

Result: `{A: w_A, B: w_B, C: w_C, D: w_D}`. The list is sorted by creator id ascending — call it `[w_A, w_B, w_C, w_D]`. `minNGen` and `minBirthRound` are folded over the list; `setJudgeTrue` flips the flag on each. `findAllJudges` returns the four-event list.

`checkJudges` sums roster weights: 1 + 1 + 1 + 1 = 4. `Threshold.SUPER_MAJORITY.isSatisfiedBy(4, 4)` is true; nothing is logged. `generateWhitening` XOR-folds the four base hashes into a `whitening` byte array; call it `W = h(w_A) ^ h(w_B) ^ h(w_C) ^ h(w_D)`.

`findConsensusEvents` runs `search.commonAncestorsOf([w_A, w_B, w_C, w_D], nonConsensusNonAncient)`. Suppose the DAG contains:

- `e1`: a round-3 event by D, ancestor of all four judges (typical for events by long-running honest creators well before the round bump).
- `e2`: a round-4 event by A on the *honest* side of A's fork that ancestors `w_A`, `w_B`, `w_C`, `w_D` but *not* `w_A'`. Since `w_A'` is not in the judge list (it lost to `w_A` in the merge), this is an ancestor of every judge.
- `e3`: a round-4 event by C that descends from `w_B`, `w_C`, `w_D` but does not descend from `w_A` (its parent line never reached A's witness). Ancestor of three judges; not in the return set.

The return set includes `e1` and `e2` and not `e3`. Each gets `setIsConsensusTrue(e, 5)` — `roundReceived = 5`, `preliminaryConsensusTimestamp` computed from `recTimes`, `isConsensus = true`. `e3` keeps `isConsensus = false` and waits for a future round in which it ancestors all judges.

`ConsensusSorter.sort([e1, e2], W)` runs. Each event gets a `cGen` assigned. Sort keys: preliminary consensus timestamp first; suppose `e1.preliminaryConsensusTimestamp` is earlier — `e1` precedes `e2` and the sort terminates. The whitening only matters in the rare path where the first three keys all tie, which does not happen here.

`numConsensus` is incremented once per consensus event. `lastConsensusTime` is updated to the last transaction's timestamp in the last consensus event. The judge IDs are built — `[(A.id, h(w_A)), (B.id, h(w_B)), (C.id, h(w_C)), (D.id, h(w_D))]` — and folded into a `ConsensusSnapshot(round=5, minimumJudgeInfoList=…, numConsensus, toPbjTimestamp(lastConsensusTime), judgeIds=…)`. The `ConsensusRound` returned wraps the consensus-events list, the `EventWindow(currentRound=5, pendingRound=6, ancientThreshold=…, expiredThreshold=…)`, the snapshot, the active roster, the `pcesMode` flag, and `time.now()`. Downstream — covered in cluster C — the snapshot writes to disk and the round flows into signed-state hashing.

The Byzantine takeaway: A's branching cost A nothing in terms of *consensus correctness* — the round still decided cleanly, every honest node arrived at the same judge `w_A`, and `e2` (which descended from A's honest witness) reached consensus normally. What A's branching *did* cost was a multi-round delay in voting. If A had stayed honest, fame on round 5 likely decides at round difference 2 — fast counting-vote convergence. With A branching, the deadlock pushed the decision past the coin round and into round 18. The price of malice is paid in liveness latency, not in consensus violation. The judge structure absorbs the malice without forking the consensus output.

## Comprehension prompt

Two openings to take into the tutor chat. First: `uniqueFamous` resolves a branched creator's competing famous witnesses by picking the one with the lexicographically-least signed-byte base hash. Why is *base hash* the right discriminator versus alternatives like creation timestamp, NGen, or birth round — what property of the base hash makes the merge Byzantine-safe across honest nodes that may have observed the fork in different orders? Second: `checkJudges` logs an error and continues if the judge weight does not clear super-majority or if the judge list is empty. Walk what an adversary would need to do to drive a real round into either of those states, then argue whether the soft-fail choice is correct given that consensus correctness is downstream of fame voting (which already has Byzantine safety properties baked in by A.1-4) — and what the alternative `throw`-and-halt design would and would not buy.
