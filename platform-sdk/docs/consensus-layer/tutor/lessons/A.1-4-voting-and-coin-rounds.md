# Voting and coin rounds

## Where we are

This is the fourth lesson in cluster A.1 (Hashgraph Algorithm). [A.1-1 the hashgraph DAG](A.1-1-hashgraph-dag.md) defined the data structure; [A.1-2 rounds and witnesses](A.1-2-rounds-and-witnesses.md) wrote a *round-created* number onto every vertex and identified *witnesses* as the events that mark a round transition; [A.1-3 strongly-seeing](A.1-3-strongly-seeing.md) opened the per-event, per-member predicate that round assignment and fame voting both lean on. This lesson takes strongly-seeing into the second of its two consumers. We define first votes and counting votes, walk the recursion that takes them across rounds, and explain why coin rounds — a periodic non-deciding round driven by event signatures — are the load-bearing piece that keeps fame elections from being held open forever by an adversarial schedule. The next lesson [A.1-5 judges](A.1-5-judges.md) collapses the famous witnesses this lesson decides into the unique-per-creator set that fixes round-received for ancestor events.

## Motivating problem

Witnesses alone do not decide a round. Round assignment, covered in A.1-2, only labels each witness with the round it created; it does not say which of those witnesses are *famous*, and round-received — the per-event consensus round assignment that A.1-5 will pin down — is defined against the famous witnesses, not all of them. Nor do witnesses decide their own fame: fame is a downstream judgment by *later* witnesses about whether an *earlier* witness is widely seen across the network.

The algorithm needs that downstream judgment to be three things at once. *Deterministic*, so every honest node computes the same fame verdict from the same DAG without exchanging any vote messages — the [voting concept](../../concepts/voting.md) is *virtual* for that reason. *Byzantine-safe*, so a faction of up to a third (by weight) of the roster cannot force a wrong verdict or split honest nodes onto disagreeing verdicts. And *live*, so an adversary that controls scheduling — choosing when events are released, when gossip pairs sync — cannot keep an honest fame question undecided forever.

The first two requirements — determinism and Byzantine safety — are met by tallying weight over the witnesses each voter strongly sees and gating decisions on a super-majority threshold. That alone is not enough for liveness. A scheduler that arranges votes to land just shy of super-majority on both YES and NO can hold a single witness's fame open across arbitrarily many voting rounds. Coin rounds are the algorithm's answer: every Nth round, votes are forbidden from deciding and a stalled vote falls through to a deterministic-pseudo-random bit drawn from the voter's own event signature. The classical analysis turns that periodic randomization into a probabilistic-termination guarantee. This lesson covers both the voting machinery and the coin-round escape, anchored in `ConsensusImpl`.

## Concept

The conceptual definitions are at [`concepts/voting.md`](../../concepts/voting.md) and [`concepts/coin-rounds.md`](../../concepts/coin-rounds.md). Read both before continuing.

The four framings to anchor on:

- **Election scope.** At any moment one round is "the election round" — the lowest round whose witnesses still have undecided fame. Witnesses in any *later* round are voters; each candidate witness in the election round is voted on by every later witness until decided. The bookkeeping of who is undecided lives on `RoundElections` (witnesses and decided-fame status; [`RoundElections.java`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/RoundElections.java)).
- **First vote vs counting vote.** A voter at round `r + d` casting a vote on a candidate in round `r` looks at `d`. If `d == 1` the voter is one round above the candidate; the vote is a *first vote*, decided by walking the voter's first-self-witness chain to ask whether the candidate is the first round-`r` witness by its creator that the voter sees. If `d > 1` the voter is two or more rounds above; the vote is a *counting vote*, decided by tallying the votes the voter's strongly-seen round-`(d - 1)` witnesses have already cast on the candidate.
- **Decision gate.** A counting vote *decides* fame iff the YES-weight or the NO-weight reaches `Threshold.SUPER_MAJORITY` (strictly more than two-thirds of total roster weight, [A.1-3](A.1-3-strongly-seeing.md#how-it-works) walked the threshold). Either side. Once any voter casts a deciding vote on a candidate, the candidate's fame is fixed for the run.
- **Coin-round escape.** When the round difference `d` is a multiple of the configured coin frequency, the voter's vote *cannot* decide fame. If the strongly-seen tally reached super-majority, the voter follows it; if it did not, the voter falls through to a pseudo-random bit from its own event signature. The non-deciding constraint and the random fallback together ensure that adversarial schedules cannot indefinitely starve honest super-majorities — every coin round is a coin flip the adversary cannot control in advance.

Two structural facts these framings impose. First, the recursion is *strict on round difference*: a voter at round difference `d` consults *only* its strongly-seen witnesses at round difference `d - 1` — it does not look further back, and it does not look at peer voters at the same `d`. Voting is monotone in vote layers, and that lets the recursion be evaluated event-by-event in topological order without revisiting earlier rounds. Second, the coin round's role is *liveness*, not fairness — the coin's value does not bias the verdict, only breaks the deadlock so that subsequent counting rounds have a varied input distribution to find super-majority on. A coin round whose strongly-seen tally already reaches super-majority simply follows the tally; the random fallback only fires when the tally is split.

## How it works

The voting driver is `voteInAllElections` at [`ConsensusImpl.java#L506`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L506). It runs once per voting witness — every witness whose round is greater than the election round, immediately after `addEvent` confirms the event is a witness ([`ConsensusImpl.java#L398`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L398)). The voter casts one vote per undecided candidate in the election round and writes the result into per-event vote slots that later voters at higher rounds will tally.

The driver computes the round difference `diff = round(votingWitness) - electionRound` ([`ConsensusImpl.java#L509`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L509)) and dispatches:

- *First-vote pass.* `diff == 1`. Lines [514–522](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L514). The driver iterates undecided candidates and calls `firstVote(votingWitness, candidateWitness)` per candidate. First votes never decide; they only seed the layer the next round's counting votes will tally over.
- *Counting-vote pass.* `diff > 1`. Lines [524–563](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L524). Before iterating candidates, the driver fetches `getStronglySeenInPreviousRound(votingWitness)` — the witnesses in the voting witness's parent round that it strongly sees, gathered by walking each roster member through `stronglySeeS1` ([`ConsensusImpl.java#L683`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L683)). This list is the input to every counting vote in the pass: each strongly-seen witness already has a vote slot for every candidate in the election round, deposited when *it* was processed at lower `diff`. The driver then iterates undecided candidates, computing a `CountingVote` per candidate and either applying it or falling through to the coin branch.

`firstVote(voting, votedOn)` at [`ConsensusImpl.java#L661`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L661) implements the round-difference-1 rule. It looks up `firstSee(voting, rosterIndex(votedOn.creator))` — the first witness by the candidate's creator that lies on the voter's self-ancestor chain — and walks self-parents back through `firstSelfWitnessS` while the round is too high, until it lands on a round-`r` witness or runs out of self-parents. The vote is true iff that witness equals the candidate. The structural read: a first vote is YES iff the candidate is the first witness by its creator that the voter directly sees on its own self-line; it is NO if the voter sees a different witness by that creator first (typical for branching creators) or sees no witness by that creator at the right round at all. First votes do not decide; the driver records them and moves on.

`getCountingVote(candidateWitness, stronglySeen)` at [`ConsensusImpl.java#L587`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L587) is the tally proper. It walks every witness in the strongly-seen list, reads that witness's prior vote on the candidate (set when the witness was itself processed by `voteInAllElections` at one lower `diff`), and accumulates the creator's roster weight into `yesWeight` or `noWeight`. The function returns a [`CountingVote`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/CountingVote.java) — one of `YES_MAJORITY`, `YES_MINORITY`, `NO_MAJORITY`, `NO_MINORITY` — packing two booleans:

- `vote` — `yesWeight >= noWeight`. The direction the voter casts. The tie-break favors YES.
- `supermajority` — true iff `Threshold.SUPER_MAJORITY.isSatisfiedBy(yesWeight, totalWeight)` *or* `…isSatisfiedBy(noWeight, totalWeight)` ([lines 599–601](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L599)). The flag is *either-side* super-majority — YES super-majority and NO super-majority both flip it to true. The class JavaDoc on `CountingVote.get` reads as if `supermajority` means YES super-majority specifically; the in-code computation and the use site treat it as the deciding gate regardless of side. Open question on docstring tightening below.

Back in `voteInAllElections`, the dispatch on the `CountingVote` divides into two branches, controlled by `isCoinRound(diff)` at [`ConsensusImpl.java#L613`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L613) — `diff % config.coinFreq() == 0`, where `coinFreq` defaults to 12 ([`ConsensusConfig.java#L23`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/config/ConsensusConfig.java#L23)):

- *Normal counting branch.* Lines [549–562](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L549). The voter writes `countingVote.getVote()` into its slot for the candidate. If `countingVote.isSupermajority()` is true, the candidate's fame is decided that side: `candidateWitness.fameDecided(vote)` flips the candidate to decided famous (or decided not-famous) on the voter's pass, and if every candidate in the election round is now decided, `RoundElections.isDecided()` becomes true and the driver returns early so the caller can run round-decided.
- *Coin branch.* Lines [533–547](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L533) — `coinVote(votingWitness, candidateWitness, countingVote)`. The voter writes a vote but does not call `fameDecided`, so coin rounds cannot decide. The vote written is `countingVote.getVote()` if the strongly-seen tally already hit super-majority either side, else `ConsensusUtils.coin(votingWitness.getBaseEvent())` — the parity of the voter's stored coin field on its `EventCore` ([`ConsensusUtils.java#L28`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusUtils.java#L28)). The function comment frames the security floor: the coin does not need to be cryptographically strong, only unpredictable enough that an adversary cannot reliably steer the bit before seeing the event.

Two structural points the dispatch turns on. First, the coin branch *always* writes a vote, whether or not the tally was super-majority — the random fallback only fires on a split tally, but the deterministic super-majority pass-through still happens, so a coin round in the lucky case where every voter sees consistent strongly-seen votes simply propagates the answer one round forward without deciding it. Second, the `coinFreq == 1` configuration would make every voting round a coin round, never decide, and never reach a famous witness. The default 12 means rounds 1, 11, 13, 23, 25 … above the election round are normal counting rounds and rounds 12, 24, 36 … are coin rounds — eleven counting attempts between each coin escape. The configuration is held in `ConsensusConfig`; SME confirmation that production deployments leave `coinFreq` at the default is in Open questions.

A few wiring details to round out the mechanism. `voteInAllElections` is the *only* call site that ever sets fame. It runs on every new witness above the election round; rounds advance when `RoundElections.isDecided()` flips, after which `roundDecided` packages the result (covered in [A.1-5 judges](A.1-5-judges.md)). When a witness's round equals the election round itself, `voteInAllElections` is *not* called for it — instead `rounds.newWitness(event)` registers it as an additional candidate in the round being voted on ([`ConsensusImpl.java#L394`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L394)). And when a witness's round is below the election round (the round was already decided before this witness arrived), it is immediately marked not-famous and fame-decided without ever entering the voting machinery ([`ConsensusImpl.java#L400-L404`](https://github.com/hashgraph/hiero-consensus-node/blob/main/platform-sdk/consensus-hashgraph-impl/src/main/java/org/hiero/consensus/hashgraph/impl/consensus/ConsensusImpl.java#L400)) — the round's famous set is fixed at the moment the round decided, and any late-arriving witnesses for that round join the not-famous tail.

## Worked example

Four equal-weight nodes A, B, C, D; super-majority = strictly more than 2/3 of weight 4, so 3 of 4 satisfies and 2 of 4 does not. The same setup A.1-3 used.

Round 5 has four candidate witnesses, one per creator: `w_A`, `w_B`, `w_C`, `w_D`. The election round is 5. Suppose round-6 witnesses `v6_A`, `v6_B`, `v6_C`, `v6_D` exist and each one sees `w_A` as the first round-5 witness by A on its own self-ancestor chain (the typical no-branching case). When `voteInAllElections` runs on `v6_A`, `diff = 6 - 5 = 1`, the first-vote branch fires, and for each of the four candidates `firstVote` returns true iff the candidate is the first witness by its creator on `v6_A`'s self-ancestor chain. With no branching every first vote is YES. The driver writes YES into `v6_A`'s slot for each of `w_A`, `w_B`, `w_C`, `w_D`. The other round-6 witnesses do the same — first votes are not decisive, just seeded.

Now round 7 starts. `voteInAllElections` runs on `v7_A` first; `diff = 2`, the counting branch fires. The driver computes `getStronglySeenInPreviousRound(v7_A)` — for each member `m` of the roster it calls `stronglySeeS1(v7_A, m)`, returning the round-6 witness by `m` that `v7_A` strongly sees, or null. In the well-mixed case each call returns the corresponding round-6 witness, and the strongly-seen list is `[v6_A, v6_B, v6_C, v6_D]`.

Take the candidate `w_A`. `getCountingVote` walks the strongly-seen list, reading each round-6 witness's vote on `w_A`. All four voted YES on `w_A` in their first-vote pass; weights `1+1+1+1 = 4` accumulate on YES, none on NO. `Threshold.SUPER_MAJORITY.isSatisfiedBy(4, 4)` is true, so the returned `CountingVote` is `YES_MAJORITY`. Back in the driver, `isCoinRound(2)` is `2 % 12 == 0` — false. The normal branch fires: the slot is written YES, `candidateWitness.fameDecided(true)` flips `w_A` to decided famous, and the same pass repeats for `w_B`, `w_C`, `w_D`. Each one decides famous. After the fourth, `RoundElections.isDecided()` returns true; the driver exits, and the caller invokes `roundDecided(roundElections)` for round 5.

Now alter the scenario. Suppose A is Byzantine and branches its self-line in round 5: it publishes a fork witness `w_A'` to half the network and `w_A` to the other half. Round-6 witnesses split: B and D first-see `w_A'`, C first-sees `w_A`. `v6_B` and `v6_D` write YES on `w_A'` and NO on `w_A` (because their `firstSee` lands on `w_A'`, so `firstVote(v6_B, w_A) == false`). `v6_C` writes YES on `w_A` and NO on `w_A'`. `v6_A`'s first vote depends on the path A's own self-witness chain takes; assume it lands on `w_A`, so `v6_A` writes YES on `w_A`, NO on `w_A'`.

Round 7 votes count over round 6. Take `v7_A` again, strongly-seeing all four round-6 witnesses. For candidate `w_A`: `v6_A` YES, `v6_B` NO, `v6_C` YES, `v6_D` NO. Yes-weight 2, no-weight 2. Tie. `Threshold.SUPER_MAJORITY.isSatisfiedBy(2, 4)` is false on both sides; super-majority is false. The `CountingVote` is `YES_MINORITY` (the tie-break `yesWeight >= noWeight` favors YES). The normal branch writes YES into `v7_A`'s slot but does *not* call `fameDecided` — without super-majority the candidate stays undecided. The same split happens for `w_A'`. Round 7 votes seed round 8; round 8 counts round 7; rounds 8 through 11 keep tallying without deciding because the underlying round-6 disagreement is structural and propagates forward.

At round 17, `diff = 17 - 5 = 12`, `isCoinRound(12) == true`, the coin branch fires. For candidate `w_A`, suppose the round-16-strongly-seen tally is again split 2/2 (the disagreement has not been resolved by mixing). `getCountingVote` returns a non-super-majority result. `coinVote` then writes `ConsensusUtils.coin(v17_A.getBaseEvent())` — the parity of `v17_A`'s coin field — without flipping fame. Different round-17 voters consult their *own* event signatures, so their coin flips diverge. The diverging round-17 votes feed round 18's counting tally; with the round-17 distribution now driven partly by the coin, the chance that round 18's strongly-seen tally on `w_A` clears super-majority on either side is probabilistically positive. If it does, fame on `w_A` decides at round 18; if not, voting continues to round 23 (counting), round 24 (coin again), and so on. The classical analysis shows the probability of *not* deciding by round `r + 12k` decays exponentially in `k`.

The structural takeaway: in the well-mixed honest-only case fame typically decides by round difference 2 — one first-vote layer plus one counting layer. The coin round's role is the pathological case where adversarial scheduling or honest-but-unlucky network mixing leaves the strongly-seen tally split for many rounds in a row. The coin escape has probabilistic but bounded-in-expectation termination, which is what the algorithm needs.

Two openings to take into the tutor chat. First: `getCountingVote` returns super-majority true when *either* yes-weight or no-weight clears the threshold, and the driver flips fame on either side via `candidateWitness.fameDecided(votingWitness.getVote(candidateWitness))`. Why is it sound for the algorithm to commit to "decided not famous" on a NO super-majority — what would go wrong if the algorithm only allowed YES super-majority to decide, and how is the symmetry between the two outcomes mirrored in the round-received logic that A.1-5 will cover? Second: the coin round runs `ConsensusUtils.coin` on the voter's *own* event signature, not on the candidate's. Walk what an adversary who controls a third of the roster's weight would need to do to bias many coin votes the same way at a single coin round, and what the per-voter dependence on the voter's own signature buys against that attack. Compare against an alternative coin source — a hash of the candidate's signature, or a hash of the round number — and identify what each alternative would weaken.
